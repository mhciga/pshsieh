Engaging Activity of Accessibility - Disabilities Personas

Overview

The purpose of this engaging activity is to help learners understand the learning situations and barriers faced by disabled students in online courses, and encourage their active engagement in inclusive design practices. This is a learning approach utilizes personas to help the learners relate to the experiences of disabled individuals in online learning. The learners are tasked with reviewing the personas, considering potential solutions to address various learning barriers caused by disabilities, and incorporating inclusive design thinking to refine the online course design.

A total of 12 personas, which are developed using Generative AI tool Microsoft Bing (Microsoft Designer), represent a variety of disabilities. These disabilities are categorized into three main groups: permanent, temporary, and situational, which expand our understanding of disability. Each category encompasses four disability symptoms: inability to see, hear, speak, and touch. These 12 personas represent individuals with diverse backgrounds and different types of disabilities.

Challenge

As online learning has become a crucial educational approach in the post-pandemic era, prioritizing accessibility has become essential in online course design. Additionally, major tech companies have advocated for the concept of "Inclusive Design" to raise awareness about accessibility. However, improving accessibility practices in online course design is not a simple task. Unfortunately, many faculty members and instructional designers in higher education do not consider accessibility as their responsibility. Consequently, numerous online courses contain inaccessible content and features.

Solution

I collaborated with the accessibility expert from eLearning Design and Services at Indiana University to design and develop 12 personas representing individuals with disabilities. These personas serve as a tool for learners to understand and empathize with the learning barriers faced by disabled individuals. They also encourage learners to reflect on the fact that anyone, including themselves, could experience disability in specific situations.

Process

  • Backward Design

    • The first step in applying backward design is to establish a desired learning outcome. Therefore, we determined that the goal of this learning activity would be to assist faculty members and instructional designers in understanding the learning situations and barriers faced by disabled students in online courses, and to encourage active engagement in inclusive design practices.

    Secondly, we needed to determine acceptable evidence for achieving the goal, so we listed several learning objectives as follows:

    1. Learners can define permanent, temporary, and situational disabilities.
    2. Learners can identify examples of permanent, temporary, or situational disabilities.
    3. Learners who are not permanently disabled can describe their own experiences regarding temporary and situational disabilities.
    4. Learners can offer potential solutions when students encounter permanent, temporary, or situational disabilities in online courses.
    5. Learners can apply inclusive design thinking to develop online courses.

    Next, we proceeded to design and develop instructions based on the learning objectives.

  • Case-Based Learning

    • To help the learners relate to the learning and life experiences of disabled people, we decided to apply case-based learning to this activity. With case-based learning, students develop skills in analytical thinking and reflective judgment by reading and discussing complex, real-life scenarios (University of Michigan, 2021). We designed and developed 12 cases, illustrating 12 individuals representing different backgrounds and disabilities. The learners are tasked with reviewing the cases, considering potential solutions to address various learning barriers caused by disabilities, and incorporating inclusive design thinking to refine the online course design.
    The 12 cases are listed below:

    1. Karen, 78, White-American, female, totally blind.
    2. Alexia, 19, African-American, female, totally deaf.
    3. Carlos, 29, Hispanic-American, gay, totally mute.
    4. Nolan, 32, White- American, male, permanent paralyzed.
    5. Brenda, 50, Asian-American, female, temporary blind.
    6. Michael, 20, African-American, male, temporary deaf.
    7. Ryan, 44, White-American, male, temporal mute.
    8. Mariana, 30, Hispanic-American, female, temporal arm injury.
    9. Daniel, 16, White-American, male, situational vision disability.
    10. Himari, 24, Japanese, female, situational hearing disability.
    11. James, 61, White-American, male, situational speaking disability.
    12. Parvati, 43, Indian, transgender, situational disability in touching.

  • Persona

    • The personas we created followed the instructions of usability.gov. We also referred to the examples provided by the Assistive Technology & Accessibility Centers (ATAC), Indiana University. The elements of each persona included:

    1. Fictional name
    2. Casual pictures representing that user group
    3. Persona Group (i.e. college student, international student)
    4. Job titles and major responsibilities
    5. Demographics such as age, education, ethnicity, and family status
    6. The goals and tasks they are trying to complete using the site
    7. Their physical, social, and technological environment
    8. Technology using experiences

    Based on these elements, we created 12 different disability personas which covered diverse backgrounds.

Results

The personas could be applied to any accessiblity related training for people who need to promote awareness of online learning accessibility, for example, faculty members as well as course design teams in higher education institutions.
